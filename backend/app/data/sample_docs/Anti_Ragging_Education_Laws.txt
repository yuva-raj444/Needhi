ANTI-RAGGING LAWS AND EDUCATION RIGHTS IN INDIA
=================================================

PART 1: ANTI-RAGGING REGULATIONS (UGC, 2009)
=============================================

WHAT IS RAGGING?
Ragging means any act that causes physical or psychological harm, raises fear or
apprehension in a fresher or junior student, including:
- Teasing, embarrassing, humiliating a student
- Forcing to do something against will (physical tasks, acts, performances)
- Abusing, using filthy language
- Assaulting, hitting, causing bodily harm
- Stripping, sexual harassment
- Making students do menial tasks or run errands
- Forcing to consume alcohol, drugs
- Preventing from studying or attending classes

SUPREME COURT ORDER — Vishwa Jagriti Mission vs Union of India (2001):
The Supreme Court directed all educational institutions to take strict action
against ragging. The Court held that ragging violates the fundamental right to
dignity and education under Articles 21 and 21A of the Constitution.

UGC REGULATIONS ON CURBING THE MENACE OF RAGGING IN HIGHER EDUCATIONAL
INSTITUTIONS, 2009:

Regulation 3 — What constitutes ragging:
Any act of physical or mental abuse (including bullying and exclusion) targeted
at another student (fresher or otherwise) on the ground of colour, race,
religion, caste, ethnicity, gender (including transgender), sexual orientation,
appearance, nationality, regional origins, linguistic identity, place of birth,
place of residence or economic background.

Regulation 6 — Duty of the institution:
Every institution must:
(a) Constitute an Anti-Ragging Committee
(b) Constitute an Anti-Ragging Squad
(c) Create an Anti-Ragging Cell
(d) Display Anti-Ragging notices prominently
(e) Obtain affidavit from every student and parent that they will not engage in ragging
(f) Maintain a helpline for complaints

NATIONAL ANTI-RAGGING HELPLINE: 1800-180-5522 (toll free, 24x7)
ONLINE COMPLAINT: www.antiragging.in

Regulation 9 — Punishments for ragging:
(a) Suspension from attending classes
(b) Withholding/withdrawing scholarship or fellowship
(c) Debarring from appearing in examinations
(d) Withholding results
(e) Debarring from representing institution in any event
(f) Suspension/expulsion from hostel
(g) Cancellation of admission
(h) Rustication from institution for 1 to 4 semesters
(i) Expulsion from institution and consequent debarring from admission to any other institution
(j) Collective punishment: if the identity of individual ragging cannot be established

CRIMINAL PUNISHMENTS FOR RAGGING:
Depending on the nature of ragging, the following IPC sections apply:
- Section 294: Obscene acts and songs — up to 3 months imprisonment
- Section 323: Voluntarily causing hurt — up to 1 year imprisonment
- Section 325: Grievous hurt — up to 7 years imprisonment
- Section 341: Wrongful restraint — up to 1 month imprisonment
- Section 342: Wrongful confinement — up to 1 year imprisonment
- Section 506: Criminal intimidation — up to 2 years imprisonment
- Section 509: Words/gestures insulting modesty of woman — up to 1 year
- Section 376: Rape — minimum 7 years imprisonment

HOW TO FILE A RAGGING COMPLAINT:
1. Call the National Anti-Ragging Helpline: 1800-180-5522
2. File complaint online at www.antiragging.in
3. Complain to the Anti-Ragging Committee of your college
4. File an FIR at the nearest police station
5. Complain to UGC at ugc@nic.in
6. Approach State/National Human Rights Commission

IMPORTANT: The institution CANNOT dismiss or ignore a ragging complaint. Failure
to act on a ragging complaint makes the institution liable.

PART 2: RIGHT TO EDUCATION ACT, 2009 (RTE)
===========================================

Article 21A of the Constitution: The State shall provide free and compulsory
education to all children aged 6 to 14 years.

RIGHT TO FREE AND COMPULSORY EDUCATION ACT, 2009 (RTE Act):

Section 3: Every child between 6-14 years has the right to free and compulsory
education in a neighbourhood school.

Section 12: Private schools must reserve 25% seats for children from
economically weaker sections (EWS) and disadvantaged groups. These children
shall be admitted free of charge.

Section 13: No school shall:
- Collect capitation fee
- Subject a child to screening procedure for admission
- Deny admission to a child

Section 17: Physical punishment and mental harassment are PROHIBITED.
Teachers who violate this shall be subject to disciplinary action.

Section 23: Teachers must have minimum qualifications as laid down by the academic authority.

Section 28: No teacher shall engage in private tuition or private teaching.

Section 31: The local authority shall ensure all children in its area attend school.

Grievance redressal under RTE:
- Complain to School Management Committee
- Complain to Block Education Officer
- Approach State Commission for Protection of Child Rights (SCPCR)
- Approach National Commission for Protection of Child Rights (NCPCR)
  Website: www.ncpcr.gov.in | Helpline: 1800-121-2830

PART 3: UNIVERSITY GRANTS COMMISSION (UGC) STUDENT RIGHTS
==========================================================

Students have the following rights in higher education:

1. RIGHT AGAINST UNFAIR FEES:
UGC regulates fees in centrally funded institutions. Private universities must
follow state government fee regulations. Refund of fees: If a student withdraws
before the academic year starts, 100% refund must be given. If within 15 days
of start, 80% must be refunded.

2. RIGHT TO SAFE CAMPUS:
Every institution must have:
- Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) for sexual harassment under POSH Act
- Anti-Ragging Committee
- Grievance Redressal Committee

3. RIGHT TO EXAMINATION RESULTS:
Students can apply for re-evaluation of answer sheets. The institution must
provide photocopies of answer sheets on request (RTI can be used for this).

4. RIGHT AGAINST WITHHOLDING DEGREE:
A university cannot withhold a degree/certificate merely because of pending
dues if the student has completed all academic requirements.
(Landmark case: Rajesh Kumar Daria vs Rajasthan PSC)

5. RIGHTS UNDER DISABILITY (RPWD ACT 2016):
Students with disabilities are entitled to:
- 5% reservation in all higher education institutions
- Scribes, extra time in examinations
- Accessible infrastructure
- Special coaching

PART 4: SEXUAL HARASSMENT IN EDUCATIONAL INSTITUTIONS
======================================================

POSH ACT (Protection of Women from Sexual Harassment at Work Place Act, 2013)
applies to educational institutions.

What constitutes sexual harassment:
- Unwelcome physical contact and advances
- Demand or request for sexual favours
- Making sexually coloured remarks
- Showing pornography
- Any other unwelcome physical, verbal or non-verbal conduct of sexual nature

Every institution with 10 or more employees must have an
Internal Complaints Committee (ICC).

Complaint procedure:
1. Written complaint to ICC within 3 months of incident
2. ICC must complete inquiry within 60 days
3. Penalty includes: warning, withholding promotion/pay rise, termination
4. Criminal complaint under IPC Section 354A also possible

PART 5: STUDENT GRIEVANCE REDRESSAL
====================================

UGC (Redressal of Grievances of Students) Regulations, 2023:

Every higher educational institution must establish a Student Grievance Redressal
Committee (SGRC).

Types of grievances covered:
- Admission
- Examination and evaluation
- Victimisation of students
- Attendance
- Harassment (including ragging and sexual harassment)
- Hostel accommodation
- Fee refund

Process:
1. First: Complain to SGRC of the institution
2. If not resolved in 15 days: Approach Ombudsman appointed by institution
3. If still not resolved: Appeal to UGC

UGC Ombudsman portal: https://www.ugc.gov.in
