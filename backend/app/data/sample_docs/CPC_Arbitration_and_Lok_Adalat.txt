CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE, 1908 (CPC) — KEY PROVISIONS

SECTION 9 — COURTS TO TRY ALL CIVIL SUITS UNLESS BARRED
The Courts shall have jurisdiction to try all suits of a civil nature excepting suits of which their cognizance is either expressly or impliedly barred.

SECTION 10 — STAY OF SUIT (RES SUB JUDICE)
No Court shall proceed with the trial of any suit in which the matter in issue is also directly and substantially in issue in a previously instituted suit between the same parties.

SECTION 11 — RES JUDICATA
No Court shall try any suit or issue in which the matter directly and substantially in issue has been directly and substantially in issue in a former suit between the same parties and decided by a Court competent to try such subsequent suit.

SECTION 12 — BAR ON SECOND SUIT ON SAME CAUSE OF ACTION
Where a plaintiff is precluded by rules from instituting a further suit in respect of any particular cause of action, he shall not be entitled to institute a suit in respect of such cause of action in any Court.

ORDER I — PARTIES TO SUITS
Rule 1: All persons may be joined in one suit as plaintiffs where the right to relief arises from the same act or transaction or series of acts or transactions.
Rule 10: The Court may add or strike out parties at any stage of the proceedings.

ORDER V — ISSUE AND SERVICE OF SUMMONS
The Court shall issue summons upon the defendant, allowing 30 days for appearance.

ORDER VI — PLEADINGS GENERALLY
Pleadings shall contain a statement of material facts. Particulars with dates and items shall be given.
Written Statement must be filed within 30 days (extendable to 90 days).

ORDER VII — PLAINT
Rule 10: Return of plaint for filing in proper court (if filed in wrong jurisdiction).
Rule 11: Rejection of plaint — The plaint shall be rejected where:
(a) it does not disclose a cause of action;
(b) the relief claimed is undervalued;
(c) insufficient stamp;
(d) the suit is barred by law.

ORDER IX — APPEARANCE OF PARTIES AND CONSEQUENCE OF NON-APPEARANCE
Rule 9: If defendant fails to appear, the Court may proceed ex parte.
Rule 13: Setting aside decree passed ex parte — if defendant satisfies the Court that summons was not duly served or he was prevented by sufficient cause from appearing.

ORDER XXXVII — SUMMARY SUIT
For suits on bills of exchange, hundies, promissory notes, or where the plaintiff seeks to recover a debt or liquidated demand. The defendant must apply for leave to defend.

ORDER XXXIX — TEMPORARY INJUNCTIONS AND INTERLOCUTORY ORDERS
Rule 1: Where the defendant threatens to dispose of or remove property, the Court may grant a temporary injunction.
Rule 2: Where the defendant threatens to do an act in relation to property, the Court may restrain the defendant.

For granting an injunction, three conditions must be satisfied:
1. Prima facie case
2. Balance of convenience in favour of the applicant
3. Irreparable injury would be caused if injunction is not granted

SECTION 80 — NOTICE BEFORE SUIT AGAINST GOVERNMENT
No suit shall be instituted against the Government until the expiration of two months after a notice in writing has been delivered to the appropriate government officer.

SECTION 89 — SETTLEMENT OF DISPUTES OUTSIDE THE COURT
Where it appears to the Court that there exist elements of a settlement which may be acceptable to the parties, the Court may refer the matter to:
(a) Arbitration
(b) Conciliation
(c) Judicial settlement including settlement through Lok Adalat
(d) Mediation

SECTION 96 — APPEAL FROM ORIGINAL DECREE
Every appeal shall be heard on the evidence recorded and the documents produced in the Court of first instance.

SECTION 100 — SECOND APPEAL
Only on a substantial question of law. No second appeal on questions of fact.

SECTION 115 — REVISION
The High Court may call for the record of any case which has been decided by any Court subordinate to it.

TERRITORIAL JURISDICTION (Section 16-20):
- Suits for immovable property: Where the property is situated
- Other suits: Where the cause of action arises, or where the defendant resides or carries on business

PECUNIARY JURISDICTION:
- District Court: Suits above a specified value (varies by state)
- Civil Judge (Senior Division): Intermediate value
- Civil Judge (Junior Division): Lower value

ARBITRATION AND CONCILIATION ACT, 1996 (As amended 2019)

SECTION 7 — ARBITRATION AGREEMENT
An arbitration agreement means an agreement by the parties to submit to arbitration all or certain disputes which have arisen or which may arise between them. It shall be in writing.

SECTION 8 — POWER TO REFER PARTIES TO ARBITRATION
A judicial authority, before which an action is brought in a matter which is the subject of an arbitration agreement, shall refer the parties to arbitration.

SECTION 9 — INTERIM MEASURES BY COURT
A party may, before or during arbitral proceedings or at any time after the making of the arbitral award, apply to a court for interim measures of protection.

SECTION 11 — APPOINTMENT OF ARBITRATORS
(1) A person of any nationality may be an arbitrator.
(2) The parties are free to agree on a procedure for appointing the arbitrator.
(3) If they fail to agree: Each party appoints one arbitrator, and the two appointed arbitrators appoint the third (presiding arbitrator).
(4) If this process fails: The Supreme Court (for international commercial arbitration) or the High Court (for domestic) shall appoint the arbitrator.

SECTION 12 — GROUNDS FOR CHALLENGE
An arbitrator may be challenged if circumstances give rise to justifiable doubts as to his independence or impartiality.

SECTION 17 — INTERIM MEASURES BY ARBITRAL TRIBUNAL
The arbitral tribunal may, at the request of a party, grant interim measures including:
(a) an order to maintain or restore the status quo pending determination;
(b) an order to take action to prevent harm or prejudice;
(c) an order to provide security for costs.

SECTION 28 — RULES APPLICABLE TO SUBSTANCE OF DISPUTE
(1) For domestic arbitration: Indian substantive law shall apply.
(2) For international commercial arbitration: Rules of law designated by parties.

SECTION 29A — TIME LIMIT FOR ARBITRAL AWARD (NEW)
(1) The award shall be made within 12 months from the date of completion of pleadings.
(2) The parties may extend the period by 6 months.
(3) Further extension only by the Court, on sufficient cause.
(4) If the award is not made within the specified time, the mandate of the arbitrator shall terminate.

SECTION 34 — APPLICATION FOR SETTING ASIDE ARBITRAL AWARD
An arbitral award may only be set aside by the Court if:
(a) a party was under some incapacity;
(b) the arbitration agreement is not valid;
(c) the party was not given proper notice;
(d) the award deals with matters beyond the scope of the arbitration agreement;
(e) the composition of the tribunal was not in accordance with the agreement;
(f) the subject matter is not capable of settlement by arbitration;
(g) the award is in conflict with the public policy of India (including patent illegality).

APPLICATION MUST BE FILED WITHIN 3 MONTHS (extendable by 30 days for sufficient cause).

SECTION 36 — ENFORCEMENT
An arbitral award shall be enforced as if it were a decree of the court.

SECTION 37 — APPEALABLE ORDERS
An appeal shall lie from:
(a) setting aside or refusing to set aside an arbitral award;
(b) granting or refusing to grant interim measures.

LOK ADALATS (Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987):
Section 19 — Organisation of Lok Adalats (people's courts)
Section 20 — Reference of cases to Lok Adalat
Section 21 — Award of Lok Adalat deemed decree of civil court; final and binding; no appeal
Advantages: No court fees, speedy justice, compromise-based settlement, legally binding
Cases that can be referred: Motor accident claims, matrimonial disputes, labour disputes, compoundable criminal cases, cases pending in courts

NATIONAL GREEN TRIBUNAL ACT, 2010:
- Established for effective and expeditious disposal of cases relating to environmental protection
- Cases related to: Forest Conservation Act, Biological Diversity Act, Environment (Protection) Act, Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act
- Provides compensation for damage to persons, property, and environment
- Appeal to Supreme Court within 90 days
