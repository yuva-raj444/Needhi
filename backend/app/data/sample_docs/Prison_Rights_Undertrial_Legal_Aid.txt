PRISON RIGHTS, UNDER-TRIAL PRISONERS AND CRIMINAL JUSTICE WELFARE
===================================================================

PART 1: RIGHTS OF PRISONERS IN INDIA
======================================

CONSTITUTIONAL RIGHTS (Cannot be waived even in prison):
1. Article 21 — Right to life and dignity (survives imprisonment)
2. Article 14 — Equal treatment of all prisoners
3. Article 22 — Right to be informed of grounds of detention, right to legal counsel
4. Article 20 — Protection against self-incrimination, double jeopardy

FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS RETAINED BY PRISONERS (Supreme Court — Sunil Batra vs Delhi Administration):
- Right to be free from cruel and unusual punishment
- Right not to be subjected to inhuman or degrading treatment
- Right against solitary confinement (allowed only as last resort)
- Right against handcuffing without justification
- Right to access medical treatment
- Right to have family informed
- Right to legal aid
- Right to meaningful communication

WHAT PRISON AUTHORITIES MUST DO:
1. Keep separate facilities for under-trial and convicted prisoners
2. Keep women prisoners separately from men
3. Keep juvenile offenders in special facilities
4. Provide adequate food, water, clothing, and shelter
5. Provide medical care
6. Allow meetings with lawyers and family
7. Respect religion and personal practices

PRISON REFORMS — SUPREME COURT DIRECTIONS:
Multiple Supreme Court cases (Re: Inhuman Conditions in 1382 Prisons — 2016):
- State must provide legal aid to all under-trial prisoners
- Periodic review of under-trial cases
- CCTV cameras in prisons
- Regular visits by District Magistrate to prisons

PART 2: UNDER-TRIAL PRISONERS AND BAIL RIGHTS
===============================================

WHO IS AN UNDER-TRIAL PRISONER:
A person arrested and detained in jail who has NOT yet been convicted.
India has one of the highest proportions of under-trial prisoners globally.

SECTION 436A CrPC (Now Section 479 BNSS):
VERY IMPORTANT RIGHT:
If an under-trial prisoner has spent HALF the maximum period of imprisonment
prescribed for the alleged offence, they have the RIGHT to bail.

Example: If maximum punishment for an offence is 6 years,
and the accused has been in jail for 3 years without conviction — 
RIGHT TO BAIL APPLIES.

EXCEPTIONS:
- Death penalty cases
- Life imprisonment cases
- Multiple pending cases

HOW TO USE SECTION 436A / 479 BNSS:
Application to the Sessions Court in the district where the case is pending.
Present a calculation of time spent in custody.
Court must release on personal bond or with surety.

SECTION 309 CrPC — SPEEDY TRIAL:
Under-trial prisoners have a right to speedy trial (Article 21).
Courts must ensure cases are not endlessly adjourned.
Hussainara Khatoon vs State of Bihar (1979): Landmark case — Supreme Court
held that right to speedy trial is fundamental right under Article 21.

PART 3: LEGAL AID FOR PRISONERS
=================================

CONSTITUTION ARTICLE 39A:
Mandates free legal aid to ensure justice is not denied due to economic
incapacity.

LEGAL SERVICES AUTHORITIES ACT, 1987:
Provides framework for free legal aid.

FREE LEGAL AID IS AVAILABLE TO:
- All under-trial prisoners who cannot afford a lawyer
- All convicted prisoners who want to appeal
- Women prisoners in all cases
- SC/ST persons
- Victims of mass disaster
- Disabled persons
- Children

NATIONAL LEGAL SERVICES AUTHORITY (NALSA):
Website: nalsa.gov.in | Helpline: 15100
Provides free legal services and runs legal aid clinics in courts and jails.

DISTRICT LEGAL SERVICES AUTHORITY (DLSA):
Available in every district. Must be present at courts and jails.
Every prisoner can request legal aid from DLSA.

PLEA OF LEGAL AID IN COURT:
If you or your family member is arrested and cannot afford a lawyer,
tell the Magistrate at the first hearing:
"I cannot afford a lawyer. I request legal aid."
The Magistrate MUST arrange a lawyer from the legal aid panel.

PART 4: TREATMENT OF SPECIAL CATEGORIES IN PRISON
====================================================

WOMEN PRISONERS:
- Must be kept in separate female wards/jails
- Female warders must be present
- Searches of women prisoners only by female staff
- Pregnant women must receive pre-natal and post-natal care
- If convicted, pregnant women near delivery may not go to jail immediately
- Infants can stay with mother in prison (up to 6 years of age in many states)

JUVENILES IN CONFLICT WITH LAW:
- Under Juvenile Justice Act 2015
- Children below 18 years CANNOT be tried as adults (except offences under
  JJ Act for heinous crimes where 16-18 year olds may be tried as adults)
- Must be sent to Observation Home (not jail)
- Juvenile Justice Boards handle juvenile cases

ELDERLY AND SICK PRISONERS:
- Must receive adequate medical care
- Courts have power to release on bail for serious illness or medical ground
- Section 437(1)(i) CrPC: Bail can be granted if accused is very ill

MENTALLY ILL PRISONERS:
- Mental Healthcare Act 2017 requires assessment before or during trial
- Cannot be sentenced unless mental fitness is established
- Prisons must have separate facilities for mentally ill prisoners

PART 5: HABEAS CORPUS — CHALLENGING ILLEGAL DETENTION
=======================================================

WRIT OF HABEAS CORPUS:
Latin for "produce the body."
Filed to challenge any illegal detention by state or private person.

WHERE TO FILE:
- High Court (Article 226): State detention
- Supreme Court (Article 32): In extreme cases, or directly for central issues

NO FORMAT REQUIRED:
Even a postcard or letter to the High Court has been treated as Habeas Corpus
in emergencies (especially for poor persons).

TIME: Habeas Corpus is an emergency remedy — courts hear it urgently,
often the same day or next day.

PREVENTIVE DETENTION:
Government can detain a person without trial under certain laws:
- National Security Act (NSA)
- Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA)
- Conservation of Foreign Exchange and Prevention of Smuggling Activities Act (COFEPOSA)
- Unlawful Activities Prevention Act (UAPA)

CHALLENGE AGAINST PREVENTIVE DETENTION:
Habeas Corpus petition in High Court.
Government must produce the detention order and justify the grounds.
Detenu must receive grounds of detention within 5 days (Article 22(5)).

PART 6: PAROLE AND FURLOUGH
==============================

PAROLE:
Temporary release of a convicted prisoner for a specific purpose.
- Regular Parole: For emergencies (death in family, marriage of child, serious illness)
- Custody Parole: Short-term release under police escort

Who grants parole: State government / prison authorities (rules vary by state).
Application to: Superintendent of Jail or Home Department.

FURLOUGH:
Periodic release for prisoners with good conduct as a reward and for rehabilitation.
No specific reason needed unlike parole.
Duration typically 14 days.

REMISSION:
Reduction in sentence for good behavior.
All prisons have rules for remission — reduces actual time served.
Life sentence = typically 14 years actual imprisonment after remissions,
but Supreme Court has held some sentences require longer (case-specific).

PART 7: NATIONAL HUMAN RIGHTS COMMISSION
==========================================

NHRC (National Human Rights Commission):
Address: Manav Adhikar Bhawan, Block-C, GPO Complex, INA, New Delhi
Website: nhrc.nic.in | Phone: 011-24651330 | Helpline: 14433 or 1800-180-5129

WHEN TO APPROACH NHRC:
- Custodial torture or death
- Police high-handedness or fake encounter
- Violation of rights of prisoners
- Illegal detention

NHRC CANNOT:
- Take cognizance of matters older than 1 year
- Take up cases where High Court / Supreme Court proceedings are pending

STATE HUMAN RIGHTS COMMISSIONS (SHRC):
Available in each state. For state government violations.

WHAT NHRC/SHRC CAN DO:
- Recommend compensation to victim
- Recommend disciplinary action against errant officials
- Recommend prosecution
- Visit prisons and detention centres
- Make reports to government

These are recommendatory bodies — they recommend action to government.
