CRIMINAL PROCEDURE, ARREST RIGHTS, BAIL AND POLICE ACCOUNTABILITY
===================================================================

PART 1: YOUR RIGHTS WHEN ARRESTED IN INDIA
============================================

CONSTITUTIONAL RIGHTS ON ARREST (Article 22):
1. RIGHT TO KNOW GROUNDS OF ARREST:
   You MUST be informed of the reasons for your arrest.
   No one can be arrested without being told why.

2. RIGHT TO CONSULT A LAWYER:
   You have the right to consult and be defended by a legal practitioner
   of your choice. Police CANNOT deny this right.
   If you cannot afford a lawyer — FREE legal aid is available.
   Call NALSA: 15100

3. RIGHT TO BE PRODUCED BEFORE MAGISTRATE WITHIN 24 HOURS:
   After arrest, you MUST be produced before the nearest Magistrate
   within 24 hours of arrest (excluding travel time).
   If not, the arrest becomes illegal and you can be released.

4. RIGHT AGAINST SELF-INCRIMINATION (Article 20(3)):
   You cannot be compelled to be a witness against yourself.
   You do not have to answer questions that may incriminate you.
   Police CANNOT force confessions.

5. RIGHT AGAINST DOUBLE JEOPARDY (Article 20(2)):
   You cannot be tried and punished for the same offence twice.

SECTION 50 CrPC / SECTION 47 BNSS — GROUNDS OF ARREST:
Person arrested must be informed immediately of the reason.
Person arrested for non-bailable offence must be informed of right to bail.

SECTION 41 CrPC — ARREST WITHOUT WARRANT:
Police can arrest without warrant only in cognizable offences.
Even then, must record reasons in writing.
Supreme Court in Arnesh Kumar vs Bihar (2014): Police should NOT
automatically arrest — must apply their mind before arresting.

SECTION 50A CrPC — NOTICE TO FAMILY:
Police must inform a family member or friend of the arrested person
about the arrest and place of detention.

D.K. BASU vs STATE OF WEST BENGAL (1997) GUIDELINES:
Supreme Court issued mandatory guidelines for arrest:
1. Police must bear name tags with designation when making arrests
2. Memo of arrest must be prepared — witnessed by family member
3. Arrestee must be informed of right to inform a friend/relative
4. Must be examined by doctor within 48 hours
5. All documents must be updated in the police station register

PART 2: BAIL IN INDIA
======================

TYPES OF OFFENCES:
1. BAILABLE OFFENCES: Bail is a matter of right.
   Police can grant bail at the police station itself.
   
2. NON-BAILABLE OFFENCES: Bail is at the court's discretion.
   Must apply to the Magistrate/Sessions Court.

3. NON-BAILABLE AND NON-COMPOUNDABLE OFFENCES (e.g., murder, rape):
   Stricter conditions for bail.
   High Court can grant bail under Section 439 CrPC.

SECTION 436 CrPC — BAIL IN BAILABLE OFFENCE:
When a person is accused of a bailable offence, bail is a right, not
a privilege. Police and court MUST grant bail on personal bond or surety.

SECTION 437 CrPC — BAIL IN NON-BAILABLE OFFENCE:
Court may grant bail considering:
- Nature and gravity of accusation
- Antecedents of accused
- Possibility of fleeing from justice
- Safety of the community
- Health and age of the accused

SECTION 167(2) CrPC — DEFAULT BAIL (Statutory Bail):
If police fail to file chargesheet within:
- 60 days for offences punishable with death, life, or 10+ years imprisonment
- 90 days for serious offences requiring special investigation
THEN the accused has a RIGHT to get bail (called Default Bail).
This is a significant right — courts MUST grant it.

ANTICIPATORY BAIL (Section 438 CrPC):
A person who apprehends arrest can approach Sessions Court or High Court
for anticipatory bail BEFORE the arrest.
If granted, police cannot arrest without court permission.

SURETY:
Usually, two sureties (people who guarantee you will appear in court)
are required for bail. For poor persons, courts can accept personal bond.

PART 3: POLICE ACCOUNTABILITY
================================

SUPREME COURT GUIDELINES IN PRAKASH SINGH vs UNION OF INDIA (2006):
Landmark case — Supreme Court issued guidelines for police reform:
1. Fixed tenure for DGP (minimum 2 years)
2. Separation of law and order from investigation
3. Police Complaints Authority in each state/district
4. Police Establishment Board for transfers, promotions

SECTION 154 CrPC — FIRST INFORMATION REPORT (FIR):
- Police MUST register FIR for cognizable offences — no discretion.
- FIR must be read to the complainant and copy given FREE of charge.
- Lalitha Kumari vs Govt. of UP (2013): Supreme Court held — FIR
  registration is mandatory for cognizable offences. No preliminary
  inquiry is required before registering FIR.

IF POLICE REFUSE TO REGISTER FIR:
1. Write to Superintendent of Police (SP)
2. Go to Judicial Magistrate and file a complaint under Section 156(3) CrPC
3. File petition in High Court for direction to police to register FIR

SECTION 41A CrPC — NOTICE OF APPEARANCE (instead of arrest):
Police may issue a notice requiring appearance instead of arrest for
offences where the accused is not likely to abscond.
Failure to appear = can then arrest.

COMPLAINTS AGAINST POLICE:
1. Complaint to Superintendent of Police
2. Complaint to State Police Complaints Authority
3. Complaint to State Human Rights Commission
4. Complaint to National Human Rights Commission: nhrc.nic.in
5. File Writ Petition in High Court for custodial violence
6. FIR against police officer for cognizable offences committed by them

CUSTODIAL VIOLENCE AND TORTURE:
- Section 330 IPC / Section 117 BNS — Voluntarily causing hurt to extort confession: 7 years imprisonment
- Section 331 IPC / Section 118 BNS — Grievous hurt to extort confession: 10 years imprisonment
- No fundamental right to torture exists in India
- India has committed to UN Convention Against Torture (though not ratified yet)

PART 4: SEARCH AND SEIZURE RIGHTS
===================================

SECTION 100 CrPC — SEARCH OF CLOSED PLACE:
- Police must have a search warrant (issued by Magistrate) to search a house
- Search warrant must specify the place and object of search
- At least 2 independent witnesses must be present during search

WHEN POLICE CAN SEARCH WITHOUT WARRANT (Section 165 CrPC):
- Cognizable offence
- Compelling reason
- No time to get warrant
- Must record reasons in writing
- Must send report to Magistrate

YOUR RIGHTS DURING SEARCH:
- Be present or have a representative present
- Demand to see the search warrant
- Witness must be from the same locality
- All seized items must be listed in a Panchnama (seizure memo)
- You are entitled to a copy of the Panchnama

PART 5: HABEAS CORPUS AND ILLEGAL DETENTION
=============================================

WRIT OF HABEAS CORPUS:
"Produce the body" — If you believe someone is being illegally detained,
you can file a Habeas Corpus petition directly in the High Court or
Supreme Court to seek their release.

WHO CAN FILE:
- The detained person themselves
- Any person on their behalf (family, friend, NGO)
- No specific format — even a letter to the High Court is sufficient

WHERE TO FILE:
- High Court: If detention is by state authority
- Supreme Court: In most important cases, or if state court is inaccessible

PART 6: CHARGESHEET AND TRIAL RIGHTS
======================================

CHARGESHEET (SECTION 173 CrPC):
- After investigation, police file a chargesheet (police report)
- Must be filed within 60 or 90 days (see default bail section above)
- Copy of chargesheet must be given to accused

RIGHT TO FAIR TRIAL:
- Right to know charges against you
- Right to be represented by lawyer
- Right to cross-examine prosecution witnesses
- Right to present defence witnesses
- Reasonable time to prepare defence
- Trial in a language you understand (interpreter if needed)

SECTION 207 CrPC — SUPPLY OF DOCUMENTS TO ACCUSED:
All documents relied upon by prosecution MUST be supplied to accused.
This includes FIR, witness statements, forensic reports, etc.

PART 7: COGNIZABLE VS NON-COGNIZABLE OFFENCES
===============================================

COGNIZABLE OFFENCES (Schedule 1 CrPC, Part I):
- Police CAN arrest without warrant
- Police CAN investigate without Magistrate's permission
- Examples: Murder, robbery, dacoity, rape, kidnapping, riot, theft (above threshold)

NON-COGNIZABLE OFFENCES (Schedule 1 CrPC, Part II):
- Police CANNOT arrest without warrant
- Police NEED Magistrate's permission to investigate
- Examples: Assault without grievous hurt, cheating below threshold, defamation

COMPOUNDABLE OFFENCES:
Offences where victim and accused can compromise and the case can be settled.
Section 320 CrPC lists compoundable offences.
Non-compoundable offences (like murder) CANNOT be settled privately.

PART 8: PLEA BARGAINING (SECTION 265A-265L CrPC)
=================================================

- Available for offences with punishment up to 7 years
- NOT available for: Offences against women and children, socio-economic offences
- Accused can approach the Magistrate to negotiate a reduced sentence
- Must be voluntary — no coercion from police or court
- Once plea bargain is accepted, no appeal on the same grounds
